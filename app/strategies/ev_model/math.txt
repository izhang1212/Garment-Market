EV_MODEL

KEY IDEA: Simulate
We test various bids/asks with their cooresponding p_fill(bid/ask)
probabilties, and choose the bid and the ask with the highest EV_ask

VARIABLES:
- μ (fair value) estimated true price
- σ (volatility) uncertainty / price dispersion
- q (inventory) how many units you hold
- k (liquidity) how quickly trades happen
- a (aggressiveness) how aggressive we want to be

Reservation price: r = μ − αq, where:
    - r = reservation price
    - μ = fair value
    - q = inventory
    - α = penalty, set to 1% of μ per unit held (α = 0.01 * μ)
Interpretation: "The value of this item is lower if I already own too much of it"
α scales with the item's own price instead of being a flat constant 
— a $200 sneaker costs $2/unit to hold, a $600 item costs $6/unit.

EXAMPLE:
Suppose
    - FV (μ) = 246
    - Inventory (q) = 2
Then,
    α = 0.01 * 246 = 2.46
    r = 246 - (2.46 * 2) = 241.08
Because inventory is positive, the reservation price shifts below FV, making
us more eager to sell and less eager to buy.


ASYMMETRIC BID/ASK
-------------------
Earlier versions picked one spread multiplier and placed bid/ask
symmetrically around r. Since bid_EV and ask_EV don't share any variables,
the model now searches for the best bid and the best ask independently —
two separate 1-D optimizations instead of one.

For each side, a multiplier m is swept via golden-section search over
m ∈ [0.1, 6.0]:
    bid = r - m_bid * σ / 2     (floored so it never drops below the min
                                  spread or $1)
    ask = r + m_ask * σ / 2

Fill probability and EV are still measured from FV, not r:
    P(fill_bid) = exp(-a * |bid - μ| / σ)
    P(fill_ask) = exp(-a * |ask - μ| / σ)
    bid_EV      = (μ - bid) * P(fill_bid)
    ask_EV      = (ask - μ) * P(fill_ask)

m_bid and m_ask are chosen separately to maximize their own side's EV —
there's no requirement that they match. 
When long (q > 0), r < μ, so the ask already sits closer to FV than the bid does, and each side can additionally
widen or tighten on top of that skew. total_EV = bid_EV + ask_EV at the
combined optimum of both sides.

EXAMPLE:
Suppose r = 241.08, σ = 5, a = 1.0, μ = 246, and the search finds
m_bid* = 1.8, m_ask* = 1.2. Then:
    bid = 241.08 - 1.8*5/2 = 236.58   →  P(fill_bid) = exp(-|236.58-246|/5) ≈ 0.15
    ask = 241.08 + 1.2*5/2 = 244.08   →  P(fill_ask) = exp(-|244.08-246|/5) ≈ 0.68
The ask is closer to FV and fills more easily — exactly the inventory
correction we want when long.

The optimizer also returns five "showcase" candidates per side (multiplier
swept in fixed steps around the optimum) so the chosen multiplier can be
displayed alongside its neighbors.

================================================================================
EV MODEL
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
The EV model is a heuristic approach. It does not derive an optimal spread from
a formula. Instead, it simulates many candidate spreads and picks the one with
the highest expected value. 
Because the bid and ask are centered around the
reservation price (not fair value), and fill probability is measured from fair
value, the two sides end up at different distances from FV. This means bid_EV
and ask_EV are naturally different — the model implicitly penalizes taking on
more inventory in the direction you are already loaded.


PARAMETERS (inputs we provide)
-----------
- transactions:         historical trade records (price + timestamp)
- decay_lambda (λ):     how fast old transactions lose relevance
- spread_multipliers:   list of candidate spread widths to try (e.g. [1.0, 1.5, 2.0])
- inventory (q):        how many units we currently hold
- inventory_penalty (α): how aggressively to shift quotes for inventory
- aggressiveness:       how steeply fill probability falls as quotes move away from FV
- min_spread:           floor on the spread — never quote tighter than this


VARIABLES (derived from parameters)
---------
Fair Value (FV):
    FV = (∑ w_i * x_i) / ∑ w_i,  where  w_i = e^(-λ * t_i)
    Recency-weighted average of transaction prices. Recent trades carry more weight.

Volatility (σ):
    σ = sqrt( (∑ w_i * (x_i - FV)²) / ∑ w_i )
    Recency-weighted standard deviation around FV. Measures price uncertainty.

Reservation Price (r):
    r = FV - α * q
    Our internal valuation adjusted for inventory. Excess inventory (q > 0)
    lowers r, shifting our quotes downward to encourage selling.


SIMULATING THE BEST BID/ASK
----------------------------
For each candidate spread_multiplier k, we generate a quote pair and score it.
There is no formula that tells us the right k — we try all of them and pick the best.

    spread   = max(k * σ,  min_spread)

    bid      = r - spread / 2
    ask      = r + spread / 2

    Note: bid/ask are symmetric around r, but NOT symmetric around FV when q ≠ 0.
    When long (q > 0), r < FV, so the ask is closer to FV than the bid is.

Fill probability — measured as distance from FV:
    P(fill_bid)  = exp(-aggressiveness * |bid - FV| / σ)
    P(fill_ask)  = exp(-aggressiveness * |ask - FV| / σ)

    A quote closer to FV is more likely to execute. Because ask is closer to FV
    when long, P(fill_ask) > P(fill_bid) — the model is more likely to sell,
    which is exactly the inventory correction we want.

Expected value per side:
    bid_EV   = (FV - bid)  * P(fill_bid)    ← profit per fill × chance of filling
    ask_EV   = (ask - FV)  * P(fill_ask)

    total_EV = bid_EV + ask_EV

The spread_multiplier with the highest total_EV is chosen. The final bid/ask
are the quotes from that winning candidate.




================================================================================
AVELLANEDA-STOIKOV MODEL
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
The AS model derives the optimal spread analytically from a continuous-time
stochastic control problem (Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation). Rather than
simulating candidate spreads, it computes exactly what the bid and ask should
be given the model's parameters. Liquidity (κ) is estimated directly from
transaction history. The optimizer sweeps over uncertain parameter values
(γ, T) and uses the same EV scoring to find the best combination.


PARAMETERS (inputs we provide)
-----------
- transactions:             historical trade records (price + timestamp), shared with EV model
- fair_value (s):           estimated true price (taken from EV model output)
- volatility (σ):           price uncertainty (taken from EV model output)
- inventory (q):            how many units we currently hold
- risk_aversion (γ):        how much we penalize holding inventory risk
- time_horizon (T):         how long we plan to be in the market
- aggressiveness:           fill probability decay rate (shared with EV model)
- min_spread:               floor on the spread


VARIABLES (derived from parameters)
---------
Liquidity (κ):
    The AS model treats market order arrivals as a Poisson process with rate κ.
    Rather than hardcoding κ, we estimate it directly from the transaction history
    using the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) for a Poisson rate:

        κ = N / W

    where:
        N = total number of transactions in the observation window
        W = width of the observation window in days
              (from the oldest transaction timestamp to now)

    This gives κ in units of trades per day. A more liquid item (many recent
    sales) produces a higher κ, which tightens the spread; an illiquid item
    (few sparse sales) produces a lower κ, which widens the spread — exactly
    the behavior the AS model intends.

    Example:
        Nike AF1:   30 sales over 25 days  →  κ ≈ 1.20  (liquid, tighter spread)
        Nike Air Mag: 15 sales over 120 days →  κ ≈ 0.13  (illiquid, wider spread)

Reservation Price (r):
    r = s - q * γ * σ² * T
    Inventory penalty scales with volatility, risk aversion, and time horizon.
    As T → 0 (end of session), r → s and inventory barely affects the quote.

Optimal Spread (δ):
    δ = γ * σ² * T  +  (2/γ) * ln(1 + γ/κ)

    Two components:
      - Risk term    [γ * σ² * T]:         wider when vol / risk aversion / time is high
      - Liquidity term [(2/γ) * ln(...)]:  wider when κ is low (few arriving orders),
                                            so each fill needs to be more profitable


FORMULAS FOR BID/ASK
--------------------
    bid  = r - δ / 2
    ask  = r + δ / 2

Unlike the EV model, the spread width is not searched — it is computed directly
from the formula above. The quotes are symmetric around the reservation price
because the model assumes buy and sell orders arrive at equal rates.

EV scoring — used by the optimizer to select the best (γ, T) combination:
    P(fill_bid)  = exp(-aggressiveness * |bid - FV| / σ)
    P(fill_ask)  = exp(-aggressiveness * |ask - FV| / σ)
    bid_EV       = (FV - bid)  * P(fill_bid)
    ask_EV       = (ask - FV)  * P(fill_ask)
    total_EV     = bid_EV + ask_EV

The optimizer sweeps all combinations of risk_aversion × time_horizon and
returns the candidate with the highest total_EV. Liquidity is no longer swept —
it is fixed at the data-derived κ.




================================================================================
DIFFERENCES
================================================================================

HOW THE SPREAD IS DETERMINED
    EV model:   no formula. Sweeps candidate spread_multipliers, picks best total_EV.
                The spread width emerges from simulation, not theory.
    AS model:   closed-form formula. δ = γσ²T + (2/γ)ln(1 + γ/κ).
                κ is computed from transaction history; γ and T are still swept.

QUOTE SYMMETRY
    EV model:   quotes are symmetric around r, but asymmetric relative to FV when
                inventory ≠ 0. The bid and ask have different fill probabilities and
                EVs — this asymmetry is what drives the inventory correction.
    AS model:   also symmetric around r, same consequence relative to FV.
                The δ/2 symmetry follows from assuming equal bid/ask arrival rates.

INVENTORY PENALTY
    EV model:   r = FV - α * q. Linear, constant penalty. α has no connection to
                volatility or time — purely a tunable number.
    AS model:   r = s - q * γ * σ² * T. Penalty grows with volatility, risk
                aversion, and time horizon. Grounded in the theory.

FAIR VALUE SOURCE
    EV model:   computes FV internally from transaction history.
    AS model:   takes FV as an input. In this codebase, it uses the EV model's output.

WHAT THE OPTIMIZER TUNES
    EV model:   spread_multiplier (1 dimension).
    AS model:   risk_aversion, time_horizon (2 dimensions, grid search).
                liquidity is no longer swept — it is derived from data.
                Both use the same total_EV criterion to pick the winner.

THEORETICAL BASIS
    EV model:   heuristic. The fill probability formula is a reasonable approximation,
                not a derived result. No proof of optimality.
    AS model:   derived from a formal stochastic control problem. Under the model's
                assumptions it produces the mathematically optimal quotes.




================================================================================
PRACTICAL USE CASES
================================================================================

WHEN TO USE THE EV MODEL
-------------------------
Use the EV model when the market does not cleanly satisfy theoretical assumptions.
Fashion resale is irregular — transactions are sparse, arrival rates are not truly
Poisson, and prices do not follow geometric Brownian motion. The EV model makes
none of those assumptions. It simply asks: "which quote produces the highest
expected profit given how likely it is to fill?" That question is robust to messy,
real-world market structure.

It is also easier to debug. If the quotes look wrong, the cause is traceable:
a spread too wide lowers fill probability, which lowers EV, which causes the
optimizer to reject it. The logic is explicit at every step.

Use the EV model when:
    - Transaction history is sparse or irregular
    - Market conditions do not fit clean theoretical assumptions
    - Transparency and debuggability matter more than theoretical elegance
    - You want a reliable baseline that degrades gracefully


WHEN TO USE THE AVELLANEDA-STOIKOV MODEL
-----------------------------------------
Use the AS model when you want the quotes to respond coherently to multiple
market conditions simultaneously. The AS spread automatically widens when
volatility is high, when inventory is large, when liquidity is low, and when
the time horizon is long — all at once, in a single formula derived from first
principles. The EV model's spread is just k * σ, so it only directly responds
to volatility. To get equivalent multi-factor behavior from the EV model you
would need to layer on additional heuristics manually.

The AS spread is also interpretable in a way the EV spread is not. The two
components — the risk term (γσ²T) and the liquidity term ((2/γ)ln(1 + γ/κ)) —
tell you exactly what is driving the width at any given moment. If the spread
widens, you can immediately attribute it to either inventory risk or liquidity
conditions.

Use the AS model when:
    - The market is active enough that Poisson arrival assumptions roughly hold
    - You need quotes that jointly respond to volatility, inventory, liquidity,
      and time horizon without manual tuning
    - Interpretability of the spread components matters
    - You want a theoretically grounded framework that scales to new items
      without recalibrating heuristics


FOR THIS PROJECT SPECIFICALLY
------------------------------
For a resale market with sparse, irregular transactions the EV model is likely
more reliable in practice — the AS assumptions are clearly violated in thin
markets like vintage or illiquid items (e.g. Nike Air Mag, Polo Bear Sweater).
AS is the stronger academic contribution and the more elegant framework, but
the EV model is more honest about what the data actually supports.

The two models are best used together: run EV as a sanity check on AS output.
If they agree, the quote is robust. If they diverge significantly, it is a
signal that the market conditions are unusual and extra caution is warranted.
